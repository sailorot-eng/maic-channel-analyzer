## THEORETICAL GROUNDING

**Occupational Agency** (Wilcock, 2006): The capacity to act purposefully and
self-directedly within one's environment to accomplish meaningful occupational goals.
In sailing, agency manifests as the sailor's ability to read, decide, and act on
the maritime environment — solo or in coordination with crew.

**Command Presence** (USCG OUPV Framework): The observable constellation of
behaviors, competencies, and dispositions that define a competent, responsible
vessel operator. Encompasses both technical seamanship and interpersonal leadership.

**Occupational Justice** (Townsend & Marval, 2013): The right of all people to
participate in meaningful occupations. In adaptive sailing, this means examining
who gets to develop agency and command presence, and what structural or attitudinal
barriers enable or inhibit that development.

## HOW TO USE THIS CODEBOOK

Each code has:
- **Code ID** — unique identifier for your analysis software
- **Definition** — what the code means
- **Positive Indicators** — what to look/listen for (present)
- **Negative Indicators** — what signals absence or inhibition
- **Example** — a concrete instance from sailing context
- **Photovoice Prompt** — instruction for adaptive sailors

Codes are organized into **5 Domains**:
  A. Occupational Agency
  B. Command Presence
  C. Occupational Identity & Meaning
  D. Occupational Justice & Adaptive Sailing
  E. Post-Work Identity

## DOMAIN A: OCCUPATIONAL AGENCY

### A1 — ENVIRONMENTAL READING
**Definition:** The sailor's demonstrated ability to assess, interpret, and
anticipate the maritime environment (wind, sea state, weather, traffic, hazards).

**Positive Indicators:**
- Scanning horizon, instruments, or sky before acting
- Verbalizing weather assessment ("wind's building from the northwest")
- Checking chart, tide tables, or weather app before departure
- Adjusting plans based on environmental change
- Discussing sea state in terms of risk or opportunity

**Negative Indicators:**
- Surprised by conditions that were foreseeable
- Ignoring instrument data
- Reacting rather than anticipating
- Delegating all environmental reading to instruments/autopilot

**Example:** "I could see the cumulonimbus building to the south. We had maybe
two hours before it hit — enough time to make the anchorage if we left now."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the moment when you first read the conditions
and knew what your plan would be."

---

### A2 — INTENTIONAL DECISION-MAKING
**Definition:** Conscious, deliberate choices made by the sailor at critical
junctures — especially under time pressure or ambiguity.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Explicitly stating the decision and its rationale
- Weighing options aloud or in narration
- Acknowledging uncertainty while still committing to a course of action
- Revisiting decisions after outcomes are known (reflection)
- Using "I decided," "we chose," "the call was" language

**Negative Indicators:**
- Defaulting to someone else's judgment without engagement
- Avoiding the decision ("let's just see what happens")
- Post-hoc rationalization without genuine reflection
- Decision paralysis in time-critical moments

**Example:** "We had two choices: push through the night and make port by morning,
or heave-to and wait out the squall. I weighed the crew's fatigue against the
weather window and decided to heave-to."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the moment just before you made an important
decision on the water."

---

### A3 — SELF-EFFICACY & COMPETENCE NARRATION
**Definition:** The sailor's expressed belief in their own ability to handle
the demands of sailing — including acknowledgment of limits.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Statements of capability grounded in experience ("I've done this before")
- Calm narration during demanding maneuvers
- Acknowledging skill growth over time
- Distinguishing between current ability and aspirational ability
- Appropriate confidence without overconfidence

**Negative Indicators:**
- Persistent self-doubt that inhibits action
- Bravado that masks genuine risk (overconfidence)
- Attributing success entirely to luck rather than skill
- Dismissing own competence ("anyone could do this")

**Example:** "Two years ago I would have been terrified in these conditions.
Now I feel the boat telling me what she needs, and I know how to respond."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph a moment on the water where you felt
genuinely competent — where your skills matched the challenge."

---

### A4 — ADAPTIVE PROBLEM-SOLVING
**Definition:** The sailor's ability to improvise, modify, or invent solutions
when standard approaches fail — especially in equipment failure or unexpected
conditions.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Describing workarounds for broken or missing equipment
- Improvising repairs underway
- Modifying technique based on conditions
- Learning from failures in real time
- Resourcefulness language ("we rigged a jury-rig," "we found a way")

**Negative Indicators:**
- Abandoning task when standard solution unavailable
- Requiring rescue or assistance for manageable problems
- Inability to adapt to changed conditions

**Example:** "The furling line chafed through at 0200, thirty miles offshore.
We hand-rolled the headsail and lashed it — not pretty, but it held until
we could fix it properly in port."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something on your boat or at the marina
that represents a problem you solved your own way."

---

### A5 — CO-OCCUPATION & COLLABORATIVE AGENCY
**Definition:** Moments where two or more people jointly exercise agency —
especially when individual capacity is limited by disability, fatigue, or
task complexity.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Explicit role distribution ("you take the helm, I'll handle the sheet")
- Non-verbal coordination (synchronized movement, gestural communication)
- Acknowledging when a task requires two people
- Adaptive co-occupation between disabled and non-disabled crew
- Trust language ("I knew she had it handled")

**Negative Indicators:**
- One person dominating while others are passive
- Failure to communicate roles during a maneuver
- Exclusion of crew members from meaningful participation

**Example:** "Brady takes the mainsheet while I steer — we've done it so many
times we barely need to talk. His hands know what my hands need."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph a moment when you and another person
accomplished something together that neither of you could have done alone."

## DOMAIN B: COMMAND PRESENCE

### B1 — SITUATIONAL AWARENESS
**Definition:** Active, continuous monitoring of vessel position, crew status,
traffic, weather, and equipment — and the ability to maintain that awareness
under pressure.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Regular position checks (GPS, visual landmarks, chart)
- Monitoring crew wellbeing during demanding passages
- Awareness of other vessels and their intentions
- Tracking multiple variables simultaneously
- Narrating awareness ("I'm watching that tanker's bearing")

**Negative Indicators:**
- Tunnel vision on one task while others deteriorate
- Surprise at vessel position or proximity to hazard
- Failing to monitor crew fatigue or seasickness

**Example:** "I knew we were three miles off the headland, the tide was
turning against us, and Karin was getting seasick. I had to factor all
of that into how hard to push."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the place on your boat where you feel
most aware of everything happening around you."

---

### B2 — VOICE & COMMUNICATION AUTHORITY
**Definition:** The quality of verbal communication under pressure — clarity,
brevity, tone, and authority without aggression.

**Positive Indicators (verbal/transcript):**
- Short, clear commands in tense moments
- Calm tone during emergencies (prosodic composure)
- Explicit briefings before maneuvers
- Debriefs after events ("here's what happened and why")
- Checking for understanding ("do you have it? good")

**Positive Indicators (visual/paralinguistic):**
- Steady voice pitch in rough conditions
- Direct eye contact during crew briefings
- Controlled breathing during emergencies
- No shouting, no hesitation

**Negative Indicators:**
- Shouting, panic voice, or profanity under pressure
- Vague commands ("do the thing with the rope")
- No briefing before complex maneuvers
- Communication breakdown during emergencies

**Example:** "All hands on deck. Brady, take the helm, steer 270.
Karin, I need you on the main sheet. We're going to gybe — ready?"

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the moment just before you gave
an important instruction to your crew or sailing partner."

---

### B3 — REGULATORY KNOWLEDGE & APPLICATION
**Definition:** Demonstrated knowledge and application of USCG/COLREGS rules,
maritime protocol, and safety regulations — especially in ambiguous situations.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Correct application of right-of-way rules
- Proper VHF radio use (channel 16, pan-pan, mayday protocol)
- Knowledge of lights and shapes
- Anchor light compliance
- Understanding of safe speed in restricted visibility
- Filing float plans

**Negative Indicators:**
- Guessing at rules in traffic situations
- Incorrect VHF procedure
- Failing to display required lights
- Anchoring in prohibited areas

**Example:** "The fishing vessel was the stand-on vessel. As the give-way
vessel under sail, I bore off to pass astern — exactly as COLREGS requires."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something on your boat that represents
a rule or safety system that keeps you and others safe."

---

### B4 — RISK ASSESSMENT & SEAMANSHIP
**Definition:** The active, ongoing evaluation of risk and the application of
seamanship principles to manage it — including conservative decision-making
when conditions exceed capacity.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Explicit go/no-go decision discussions
- Reefing before conditions demand it
- Turning back or seeking shelter when appropriate
- Discussing worst-case scenarios before departure
- Safety briefings for new crew

**Negative Indicators:**
- Proceeding despite clear warning signs
- Macho seamanship ("we'll push through")
- Failing to reef early
- No safety briefing for guests or new crew

**Example:** "The forecast showed 25 knots building to 35 by afternoon.
We could have made it — probably. But with a four-year-old aboard,
probably isn't good enough. We stayed put."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something that represents how you
keep yourself and others safe on the water."

---

### B5 — EMOTIONAL REGULATION & COMPOSURE
**Definition:** The demonstrated ability to maintain calm, clear thinking,
and effective function under fear, fatigue, conflict, or physical stress.

**Positive Indicators (verbal):**
- Calm narration during recalled emergencies
- Acknowledging fear without being paralyzed by it
- Humor as a coping mechanism in tense moments
- Explicit reflection on emotional management
- "I was scared but I knew what to do" language

**Positive Indicators (visual):**
- Steady hands and movement in rough conditions
- Controlled facial expression during crisis
- Deliberate, unhurried movement in urgent situations
- Returning to baseline after resolution

**Negative Indicators:**
- Panic that disrupts function
- Emotional contagion (spreading fear to crew)
- Freezing or avoidance behavior
- Hyperventilation, tears that impair decision-making

**Example:** "I was terrified. Genuinely. But I noticed my hands
were still doing the right things — easing the mainsheet, bearing off.
The body knew what to do even when the mind was screaming."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph a place or object on your boat
that helps you stay calm when things get hard."

---

### B6 — CREW LEADERSHIP & MENTORSHIP
**Definition:** The sailor's role in developing others' competence,
confidence, and agency — as skipper, mentor, or experienced crew.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Teaching moments captured on camera
- Explaining decisions to crew rather than just issuing orders
- Inviting crew participation in decisions
- Recognizing and praising crew contributions
- Passing the helm to less experienced crew in managed conditions

**Negative Indicators:**
- Doing everything themselves while crew watches passively
- Dismissing crew input or concerns
- No transfer of knowledge or skill to others

**Example:** "I put Karin on the helm through the anchorage entrance.
Was it faster if I did it myself? Yes. But she needed that experience,
and this was the right conditions for her to get it."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph a moment when you taught someone
something important about sailing — or when someone taught you."

## DOMAIN C: OCCUPATIONAL IDENTITY & MEANING

### C1 — SAILOR IDENTITY CLAIM
**Definition:** Explicit or implicit statements that position sailing as
central to the subject's sense of self.

**Positive Indicators:**
- "I am a sailor" vs. "I sail"
- Sailing as defining life narrative
- Sailing referenced in relation to other life roles
- Identity crisis language when sailing is threatened

**Example:** "I didn't choose sailing. Sailing chose me. I can't imagine
who I'd be without it."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something that represents who you
are as a sailor — not what you do, but who you are."

---

### C2 — MEANING & PURPOSE NARRATION
**Definition:** Explicit statements about why sailing matters — what it
provides beyond recreation or transportation.

**Sub-codes:**
- C2a: Freedom / Escape
- C2b: Challenge / Growth
- C2c: Community / Belonging
- C2d: Spiritual / Transcendent
- C2e: Economic / Livelihood
- C2f: Family / Relational

**Example:** "Out here, the noise of the world goes quiet.
It's the only place I feel completely present."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the moment or place on the water
where sailing means the most to you."

---

### C3 — TEMPORAL STRUCTURE & OCCUPATION
**Definition:** How sailing provides temporal rhythm, routine, and
structure to daily life — especially outside traditional employment.

**Positive Indicators:**
- Describing daily routines organized around sailing
- Tides, seasons, and passages as temporal anchors
- Watch schedules as occupational rhythm
- Voyage planning as purposeful future orientation

**Example:** "Our day starts with the weather forecast at 0600.
Everything else — meals, repairs, exploration — fits around
what the wind and weather allow."

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph the first thing you do every
morning when you're living aboard or sailing."

## DOMAIN D: OCCUPATIONAL JUSTICE & ADAPTIVE SAILING

### D1 — ADAPTIVE TECHNOLOGY
**Definition:** Any modification to standard equipment, technique, or
procedure that enables participation for sailors with disabilities.

**Sub-codes:**
- D1a: Helm adaptations (tiller extensions, steering aids)
- D1b: Line handling adaptations (clutches, winch modifications)
- D1c: Navigation adaptations (voice-controlled instruments, large displays)
- D1d: Mobility adaptations (accessible companionways, grab rails)
- D1e: Communication adaptations (AAC devices, visual signals)
- D1f: Sensory adaptations (tactile charts, audio instruments)

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph a piece of equipment or modification
that makes it possible for you to sail."

---

### D2 — OCCUPATIONAL INJUSTICE
**Definition:** Evidence of structural, attitudinal, or environmental
barriers that exclude or marginalize sailors with disabilities.

**Sub-codes:**
- D2a: Physical inaccessibility (docks, boats, facilities)
- D2b: Attitudinal barriers (being told you can't sail)
- D2c: Financial barriers (cost of adaptive equipment)
- D2d: Knowledge barriers (lack of adaptive instruction)
- D2e: Inspiration porn / ableist framing

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something at your marina or on your
boat that makes sailing harder than it needs to be for you."

---

### D3 — OCCUPATIONAL ENABLEMENT
**Definition:** Evidence of structures, relationships, or adaptations
that actively enable participation and agency for sailors with disabilities.

**Photovoice Prompt:** "Photograph something or someone that makes it
possible for you to be on the water."

---

## DOMAIN E: POST-WORK IDENTITY

### E1 — NON-WAGE OCCUPATION
**Definition:** Sailing positioned as a primary occupation outside of
traditional paid employment — its value derived from intrinsic rather
than economic reward.

**Sub-codes:**
- E1a: Explicit rejection of conventional work
- E1b: Alternative livelihood through sailing (YouTube, charter, etc.)
- E1c: Voluntary simplicity / downshifting narrative
- E1d: Retirement / post-career sailing identity

---

### E2 — AI & AUTOMATION RELATIONSHIP
**Definition:** How the sailor positions themselves relative to
technological automation in navigation and sailing.

**Sub-codes:**
- E2a: Autopilot as crew/partner
- E2b: AI routing tools as collaborative
- E2c: Resistance to automation (skill preservation)
- E2d: Hybrid human-machine agency

**Example:** "Otto [the autopilot] handles the steering.
That frees me to think — to actually be the captain
rather than just the helmsman."

## CODING INSTRUCTIONS FOR VIDEO ANALYSIS

### For Transcript Coding (MAIC / Claude)
1. Apply codes to specific quotes — always cite segment number
2. A single quote may receive multiple codes (e.g., B1 + B5)
3. Note frequency of each code across the transcript
4. Flag the 3 most code-dense segments for deeper analysis

### For Video Coding (Gemini / Manual)
1. Note timestamp (HH:MM:SS) for each coded moment
2. Distinguish verbal (V), visual (VI), and paralinguistic (P) evidence
3. Note duration of coded behavior (momentary vs. sustained)
4. Flag moments where transcript and video diverge

### Inter-Rater Reliability
For dissertation validity, have a second coder apply this codebook
to 20% of your data and calculate Cohen's Kappa (target > 0.70).

## COMMAND PRESENCE COMPOSITE SCORE

For each video, calculate a Command Presence Composite Score (CPCS):

| Domain | Codes | Weight |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Situational Awareness | B1 | 20% |
| Decision-Making | A2 + B4 | 25% |
| Communication Authority | B2 | 20% |
| Emotional Regulation | B5 | 20% |
| Regulatory Knowledge | B3 | 15% |

Rate each code 0–3:
- 0 = Absent
- 1 = Present but weak/brief
- 2 = Present and clear
- 3 = Strong, sustained, exemplary

**CPCS = weighted average × 33.3 (scaled to 100)**

Videos scoring 70+ are your richest teaching case studies for CTE.
